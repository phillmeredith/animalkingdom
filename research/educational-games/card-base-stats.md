# Card Base Stats — Research Reference

> Produced by User Researcher agent, 2026-03-29.
> Purpose: Replace the 50/50/50/50/50 placeholder stats on all Animal Kingdom cards
> with research-derived values on a 0–100 scale.
>
> Scale anchors applied throughout:
> - Speed 95 = Cheetah (~120 km/h). Human reference ~35.
> - Strength 100 = African Elephant (can push ~270 kg with trunk, lift ~300 kg). Gorilla ~85. Scale for body-relative strength noted where relevant.
> - Stamina 100 = Migratory arctic animals (Arctic Tern migrates 70,000 km/year — but as a bird is off this card list). Humpback Whale (~25,000 km annual migration) and Monarch Butterfly set the ceiling. Within this list, Arctic Fox and Humpback Whale represent the high end.
> - Agility 100 = most manoeuvrable animals (dragonfly is the gold standard but off-list; among these cards, Common Octopus and Bottlenose Dolphin represent the ceiling).
> - Intelligence 100 = Bottlenose Dolphin / Common Octopus (highest confirmed non-human cognition in diverse tasks).
>
> Confidence notes:
> - Domestic breeds: medium confidence. Breed-specific stats are well-documented for working dogs and sport horses; less so for pet cats and novelty breeds.
> - Wild mammals and sea animals: high confidence. Extensive peer-reviewed literature.
> - Dinosaurs: low-medium confidence. Values derived from biomechanical modelling and phylogenetic inference; acknowledge scientific uncertainty.

## Methodology notes

Speed values are mapped from published top speeds where known. Intermediate values are
interpolated. The scale is not strictly linear — the upper 20 points (80–100) are reserved
for exceptional speed outliers (Cheetah, Thoroughbred, Pronghorn level). Most animals
cluster in the 20–70 range.

Strength is assessed for body-relative power vs absolute force. Notes below indicate which
is the dominant factor per species. For the purposes of a child's game, absolute physical
presence (size + force) is used as the primary axis, making Elephant > Gorilla > Tiger.

Stamina reflects sustained aerobic capacity, migration behaviour, and recovery time between
effort bursts. Migratory species score high. Sprint-specialists (Cheetah) score low.

Agility incorporates directionality change, balance, coordination, and reflex speed. Octopus
and Dolphin are ceiling references. Ungulates (Shire Horse) score lower; felids score high.

Intelligence uses comparative cognition research: mirror self-recognition, tool use, problem-
solving in novel environments, social learning, and communication research. Uncertainty is
noted for extinct species.

## Cheetah — Cheetah

**Speed:** 95 — the anchor point; top speed 112–120 km/h; fastest land animal; can accelerate 0 to 100 km/h in 3 seconds; the 95 (not 100) reflects that this is the reference species for the scale ceiling rather than a literal maximum score
**Strength:** 38 — lean at 40–65 kg; not a powerful animal; cannot roar; often loses kills to lions and hyenas; strength is low for a big cat

**Agility:** 90 — the highest agility on land; uses tail as a rudder at full speed; can change direction almost instantly while running at 100+ km/h; the semi-retractable claws function like running spikes for grip
**Intelligence:** 55 — moderate; solitary hunters; capable problem-solvers in captivity; not noted for tool use or complex social cognition; focused on hunting-context decision-making

# LOST WORLD

> Note on confidence: All Lost World statistics are derived from palaeontological and
> biomechanical research and carry lower confidence than extant species. Values use:
> - Speed: bone structure analysis, trackways, and biomechanical modelling
> - Strength: bone density, muscle attachment site analysis, comparison with living relatives
> - Stamina: metabolic modelling, endotherm/ectotherm debate (most theropods and many
>   ornithischians are now believed to have been warm-blooded or mesothermic)
> - Agility: limb proportions, centre of mass, trackway analysis
> - Intelligence: relative brain to body ratio (EQ), brain cast analysis
>
> The ongoing ectotherm-vs-endotherm debate is the single largest source of uncertainty.
> If dinosaurs were fully endothermic (the current consensus leans toward mesothermy for
> most), their stamina values should be treated as lower-confidence estimates.

## Dinosaur — Brachiosaurus

**Speed:** 15 — estimated maximum speed 8–14 km/h; mass (up to 60,000 kg) made fast movement nearly impossible; long legs meant a single stride covered significant ground but pace was slow

## Pterosaur — Pterodactyl

## Dinosaur — T. rex

**Speed:** 35 — this is a deliberately important data point; T. rex was NOT fast — biomechanical modelling (Hutchinson & Garcia 2002; updated 2017) puts its maximum walking speed at 17–20 km/h and sprinting speed at approximately 17–29 km/h maximum; bipedal with a large body; 35 on the scale reflects that it was slower than a modern horse; this is counter-intuitive and makes great Coin Rush material
**Strength:** 96 — the reference strength for theropods; bite force estimated at 35,000–57,000 N (the highest of any land predator ever); body weight 8,000–14,000 kg; could crush bone completely; body strength for pinning prey was immense; 96 on the scale
**Stamina:** 52 — current evidence suggests mesothermy (between ectotherm and endotherm); could sustain moderate activity; not a pure sprint predator; ambush-pursuit hybrid; uncertain confidence
**Agility:** 22 — very low; bipedal but enormously heavy (up to 14,000 kg); turn radius was large; could not change direction quickly at speed; tail was a rigid counterbalance not a manoeuvring aid
**Intelligence:** 45 — the highest intelligence among theropods in this dataset; one study (2023, Herculano-Houzel) controversially suggested T. rex had primate-level neuron density, though this remains disputed; the consensus is that T. rex had high dinosaur intelligence including foraging memory, territory recognition, and possible social recognition; more intelligent than all other dinosaurs in this set but well below birds (which are theropod descendants) or mammals

*Key facts for Coin Rush questions:* top speed approximately 20–29 km/h (slower than a horse), bite force up to 57,000 N (strongest of any land predator), body weight 8,000–14,000 kg
*Key facts for Word Safari:* theropod (two-legged meat-eating dinosaur), extinct, apex predator, fossil
*Native region for World Quest:* North America (Late Cretaceous, 68–66 million years ago)
*Habitat for Habitat Builder:* Late Cretaceous coastal forest / floodplain

## Mammoth — Woolly

**Speed:** 30 — estimated maximum speed 20–25 km/h; similar to modern elephant; large body mass (4,000–6,000 kg) limits speed; not a fast animal but could cover ground steadily
**Strength:** 88 — close to African Elephant in absolute strength; body weight 4,000–6,000 kg; tusks up to 4 m long used for shovelling snow, fighting, and foraging; trunk strength equivalent to living elephant relatives; scores slightly below African Elephant on the scale due to smaller overall size
**Stamina:** 72 — strong endurance; migrated seasonally across the mammoth steppe (Europe to Siberia to North America); tracked individual mammoths show ranges of thousands of km over a lifetime; cold-adapted metabolism supported sustained activity in sub-zero conditions
**Agility:** 25 — similar to modern elephant; large body, pillar legs; poor directional agility; better than Brachiosaurus but not a manoeuvrable animal
**Intelligence:** 72 — closely related to the Asian Elephant (closer than to the African Elephant); likely shared high elephant cognition; herd behaviour, seasonal migration routing, and tool-adjacent tusk use suggest complex spatial memory and social intelligence; confidence is medium — inferred from phylogenetic proximity to living elephants

*Key facts for Coin Rush questions:* body weight 4,000–6,000 kg, tusks up to 4 m long, went extinct approximately 4,000 years ago (some island populations survived until then)

## Anomalies worth calling out in question design

These are counter-intuitive facts that make excellent educational moments:

- **T. rex was slower than a horse.** Speed 35. This surprises most children. The 2017 biomechanical study (Sellers et al.) estimates T. rex walked at ~7–9 km/h and could not safely run at high speed without breaking its legs. Maximum estimated speed is ~20–29 km/h.
- **Cheetah has the lowest stamina of all the big cats.** Stamina 32. Can only sprint for 20–30 seconds. Often loses its kill to lions or hyenas because it is too exhausted to defend it.
- **Pigs are more intelligent than dogs on several problem-solving measures.** Pig intelligence 80, Beagle 72. Gloucestershire Old Spots pigs can play video games and demonstrate mirror self-awareness.
- **Seahorse is the slowest fish.** Speed 5. Maximum speed 1.5 m per hour. It is beaten by a walking human (approximately 5 km/h = speed ~35 on this scale).
- **Koala has almost no stamina.** Stamina 15. Eucalyptus diet provides so little energy that Koalas sleep up to 22 hours per day to conserve what they have.
- **Brachiosaurus was stronger than T. rex.** Strength 95 vs 96 — but by a different mechanism. T. rex bite force was the highest of any land predator ever. Brachiosaurus absolute mass force was immense.
- **Arabian horse has higher stamina than Thoroughbred.** Stamina 92 vs 62. Thoroughbreds are bred for speed over 1–2 miles; Arabians can run 100 miles in endurance races.

## Confidence summary by category

| Category | Confidence | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| At Home (domestic pets) | Medium | Breed-specific movement data is well-documented; cognition research is extrapolated from breed group studies |
| Stables (horses) | High | Equine performance is extensively measured; endurance race data is particularly reliable |
| Farm | Medium-High | Agricultural animal research is robust; agility and stamina values are inferred from farming context |
| Wild | High | Extensive peer-reviewed wildlife literature; speed, strength, and migration data well-established |
| Sea | High | Marine biology well-studied; dolphin and octopus intelligence data is among the most robust in comparative cognition |
| Lost World | Low-Medium | Palaeontological inference; speed values have widest uncertainty range; treat all Lost World values as provisional |
